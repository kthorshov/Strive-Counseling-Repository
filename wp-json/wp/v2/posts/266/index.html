\u00e2\u20ac\u0153When I was 26, I went to Indonesia and the Philippines to do research for my first book, No Logo<\/i>.
\n I had a simple goal: to meet the workers making the clothes and
\nelectronics that my friends and I purchased. And I did. I spent evenings
\n on concrete floors in squalid dorm rooms where teenage girls\u00e2\u20ac\u201dsweet and
\ngiggly\u00e2\u20ac\u201dspent their scarce nonworking hours. Eight or even 10 to a room.
\nThey told me stories about not being able to leave their machines to
\npee. About bosses who hit. About not having enough money to buy dried
\nfish to go with their rice.<\/p>\n

They knew they were being badly exploited\u00e2\u20ac\u201dthat the garments
\nthey were making were being sold for more than they would make in a
\nmonth. One 17-year-old said to me: \u00e2\u20ac\u0153We make computers, but we don\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t know
\n how to use them.\u00e2\u20ac\u009d<\/p>\n

So one thing I found slightly jarring was that some of these
\n same workers wore clothing festooned with knockoff trademarks of the
\nvery multinationals that were responsible for these conditions: Disney
\ncharacters or Nike check marks. At one point, I asked a local labor
\norganizer about this. Wasn\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t it strange\u00e2\u20ac\u201da contradiction?<\/p>\n

It took a very long time for him to understand the question.
\n When he finally did, he looked at me like I was nuts. You see, for him
\nand his colleagues, individual consumption wasn\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t considered to be in
\nthe realm of politics at all. Power rested not in what you did as one
\nperson, but what you did as many people, as one part of a large,
\norganized, and focused movement. For him, this meant organizing workers
\nto go on strike for better conditions, and eventually it meant winning
\nthe right to unionize. What you ate for lunch or happened to be wearing
\nwas of absolutely no concern whatsoever.<\/p>\n

This was striking to me, because it was the mirror opposite
\nof my culture back home in Canada. Where I came from, you expressed your
\n political beliefs\u00e2\u20ac\u201dfirstly and very often lastly\u00e2\u20ac\u201dthrough personal
\nlifestyle choices. By loudly proclaiming your vegetarianism. By shopping
\n fair trade and local and boycotting big, evil brands.<\/b><\/p>\n

These very different understandings of social change came up
\n again and again a couple of years later, once my book came out. I would
\n give talks about the need for international protections for the right
\nto unionize. About the need to change our global trading system so it
\ndidn\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t encourage a race to the bottom. And yet at the end of those
\ntalks, the first question from the audience was: \u00e2\u20ac\u0153What kind of sneakers
\nare OK to buy?\u00e2\u20ac\u009d \u00e2\u20ac\u0153What brands are ethical?\u00e2\u20ac\u009d \u00e2\u20ac\u0153Where do you buy your
\nclothes?\u00e2\u20ac\u009d \u00e2\u20ac\u0153What can I do, as an individual, to change the world?\u00e2\u20ac\u009d<\/p>\n

Fifteen years after I published No Logo, I still
\nfind myself facing very similar questions. These days, I give talks
\nabout how the same economic model that superpowered multinationals to
\nseek out cheap labor in Indonesia and China also supercharged global
\ngreenhouse-gas emissions. And, invariably, the hand goes up: \u00e2\u20ac\u0153Tell me
\nwhat I can do as an individual.\u00e2\u20ac\u009d Or maybe \u00e2\u20ac\u0153as a business owner.\u00e2\u20ac\u009d<\/p>\n

The hard truth is that the answer to the question \u00e2\u20ac\u0153What can
\nI, as an individual, do to stop climate change?\u00e2\u20ac\u009d is: nothing. You can\u00e2\u20ac\u2122t
\ndo anything. In fact, the very idea that we\u00e2\u20ac\u201das atomized individuals,
\neven lots of atomized individuals\u00e2\u20ac\u201dcould play a significant part in
\nstabilizing the planet\u00e2\u20ac\u2122s climate system, or changing the global economy,
\n is objectively nuts. We can only meet this tremendous challenge
\ntogether. As part of a massive and organized global movement.<\/b><\/p>\n

The irony is that people with relatively little power tend
\nto understand this far better than those with a great deal more power.
\nThe workers I met in Indonesia and the Philippines knew all too well
\nthat governments and corporations did not value their voice or even
\ntheir lives as individuals. And because of this, they were driven to act
\n not only together, but to act on a rather large political canvas. To
\ntry to change the policies in factories that employ thousands of
\nworkers, or in export zones that employ tens of thousands. Or the labor
\nlaws in an entire country of millions. Their sense of individual
\npowerlessness pushed them to be politically ambitious, to demand
\nstructural changes.<\/p>\n

In contrast, here in wealthy countries, we are told how
\npowerful we are as individuals all the time. As consumers. Even
\nindividual activists. And the result is that, despite our power and
\nprivilege, we often end up acting on canvases that are unnecessarily
\nsmall\u00e2\u20ac\u201dthe canvas of our own lifestyle, or maybe our neighborhood or
\ntown. Meanwhile, we abandon the structural changes\u00e2\u20ac\u201dthe policy and legal
\nwork\u00e2\u20ac\u201dto others.\u00e2\u20ac\u009d<\/p>\n
